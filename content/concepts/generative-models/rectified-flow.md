# Rectified Flow

Rectified flow learns a transport that follows straight-line paths between a noise distribution and the data distribution, enabling fast, few-step sampling.

The straight-line interpolation is often:

$$
x_t = (1-t)x_0 + t x_1
$$

with target velocity:

$$
u_t = x_1 - x_0
$$

The model learns a velocity field that approximates this transport.

The training objective can be written as:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{RF}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{t,x_0,x_1}
\left[
\left\|
v_\theta(x_t,t) - (x_1-x_0)
\right\|_2^2
\right]
$$

where $x_0$ is sampled from the base distribution, $x_1$ from the data distribution, and $t\in[0,1]$.

Sampling solves the ODE:

$$
\frac{dx_t}{dt} = v_\theta(x_t,t),
\qquad x_0 \sim p_0
$$

from the base distribution toward the data distribution.

## Reflow

Reflow or distillation can train a new model on trajectories generated by an earlier model. The goal is to make the learned path straighter so fewer numerical integration steps are needed.

## Flow-Matching View

Rectified flow is a specific transport construction inside the broader flow-matching family. The training pair defines an interpolation path:

$$
x_t = \alpha(t)x_0 + \sigma(t)x_1
$$

and a target velocity:

$$
u_t = \frac{d x_t}{dt}
$$

For the straight path $\alpha(t)=1-t$ and $\sigma(t)=t$:

$$
u_t = x_1 - x_0
$$

The model claim should state the endpoint distributions, pairing rule, interpolation, and ODE solver budget.

## Evidence Contract

| Field | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Base distribution | What is $p_0$? |
| Data distribution | What empirical data defines $p_1$? |
| Pairing | Are endpoints paired independently, optimally, conditionally, or by teacher trajectories? |
| Path | Is interpolation linear, variance-preserving, or domain-specific? |
| Solver | Euler, Heun, adaptive ODE, or fixed-step schedule? |
| Step budget | How many function evaluations are used? |
| Metric | sample quality, validity, likelihood proxy, downstream utility, or speed |

Without this contract, "rectified flow" does not specify enough to compare papers.

## Geometry and Structured Data

Straight lines in Euclidean space can violate object constraints:

| Object | Constraint |
| --- | --- |
| molecule graph | atoms and bonds are discrete |
| conformer | bond lengths, torsions, chirality |
| protein coordinates | residue indexing, chain geometry, equivariance |
| protein-ligand pose | receptor frame, pocket constraints, clashes |
| sequence | tokens are discrete and often length-dependent |

For coordinates, the velocity should obey the same symmetry as the coordinate target:

$$
v_\theta(Rx+t,t)
=
R v_\theta(x,t)
$$

for rotation $R$ and translation $t$ when the coordinate frame is arbitrary.

## Why It Matters

- Straighter trajectories reduce the number of sampling steps.
- Closely related to flow matching and ODE-based generation.
- A practical route to fast diffusion-style samplers.

## Failure Modes

- Independent endpoint pairing may define paths that cross or average incompatible modes.
- Few-step sampling can reduce diversity even when samples look sharper.
- A Euclidean straight line may be inappropriate for constrained or geometric data.
- Reflow can improve speed while preserving teacher errors.

## Checks

- Are the learned paths actually close to straight?
- How much does reflow/distillation improve few-step quality?
- Does few-step sampling preserve diversity?
- Does the interpolation respect the object constraints?
- Is the ODE solver budget reported with sample quality?
- Are endpoint pairing and reflow data generation described?
- For molecules or proteins, are invalid intermediate or final samples counted?
