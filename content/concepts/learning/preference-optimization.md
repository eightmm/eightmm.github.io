# Preference Optimization

Preference optimization trains a model to prefer outputs that humans, users, tests, or another evaluator rank higher. It includes reward-model-based [[concepts/learning/reinforcement-learning|reinforcement learning]] and direct objectives over preference pairs.

For pairwise preference data $(y_w, y_l)$, a common loss is:

$$
\mathcal{L}
= -\log \sigma(r_\theta(x,y_w) - r_\theta(x,y_l))
$$

Here $y_w$ is the preferred output, $y_l$ is the less preferred output, and $r_\theta$ is a reward or preference score.

## Why It Matters

- Aligns model behavior with goals that are hard to specify as a loss.
- Common in the post-training stage of large generative models.
- Quality depends heavily on the preference data and reward signal.

## Direct Preference Objective

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)-style objectives compare a policy to a reference model:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{DPO}}
=
-
\mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)}
\left[
\log \sigma
\left(
\beta
\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_w\mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_w\mid x)}
-
\beta
\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_l\mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_l\mid x)}
\right)
\right]
$$

where $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$ anchors the optimized policy and $\beta$ controls how strongly the policy can move away from the reference.

## Preference Data Contract

| Field | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Prompt or task | What condition $x$ is the preference about? |
| Compared outputs | Are outputs final answers, trajectories, code diffs, molecules, poses, or plans? |
| Preference source | human, expert, model judge, verifier, benchmark, or rule? |
| Label type | pairwise, ranking, scalar score, binary accept/reject, or edit preference? |
| Distribution | generated by which policy or sampler? |
| Tie and ambiguity policy | are equal or low-confidence preferences kept? |
| Safety/public boundary | does the data contain private prompts, people, paths, or unpublished details? |

Preference optimization is only as meaningful as the comparison protocol.

## Evidence Boundary

| Claim | Required Evidence |
| --- | --- |
| better human preference | held-out preference evaluation with annotator or judge protocol |
| better task performance | task metric, execution result, verifier, or benchmark score |
| safer behavior | adversarial or policy-specific safety evaluation |
| less reward hacking | examples where optimized score and external quality agree |
| stable post-training | KL/reference drift, regression checks, and capability retention |

Do not equate higher preference-model score with true quality unless external evaluation supports it.

## Checks

- Are preference labels consistent and free of annotator bias?
- Does optimization reward-hack the proxy instead of the true objective?
- How much does the policy drift from the reference model?
- Is the preference signal collected from realistic tasks or artificial comparisons?
- Does the evaluation use fresh prompts not used for preference collection?
- Are win rates paired, confidence-bounded, and judged with a stated protocol?
